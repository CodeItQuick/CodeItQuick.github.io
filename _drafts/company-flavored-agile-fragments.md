# Every Company Grows Its Own Agile

On our team, product owners write the epics and the requirements on those epics — but developers write the majority of the stories underneath them. Product owners also frequently revise the stories after they're created, once the technical shape of the work becomes clearer.

---

POs understand the why and the what — they own the acceptance criteria — but they lack the technical context to write the details of the epic. So a PO brings an idea to the developer team, and it's on the developers to write out a "detailed" plan to execute on. One of the most valuable parts of this process isn't the plan itself — it's the byproduct: figuring out the general scope, finding ways to go "sooner not faster," and surfacing other ways to deliver the same value earlier.

---

We also work in milestones — another idea I haven't seen named in agile anywhere. Milestones let us segment value: after each one, we can decide whether to just stop there, or keep building to better iron out the details of a particular feature or enhancement.

---

In practice, we've never actually stopped at a milestone and concluded "that's enough value." What milestones really do is tell us when we should be demoing a feature to the broader group — a checkpoint for showing we added something to the software, not a genuine stopping point.

---

That's a feature, not a bug. Stakeholders don't expect us to stop early — they generally want everything delivered that the customer is asking for. But if we can show progress early, that's likely to make the customer happy sooner, even if the full scope is still coming.

---

One piece of generic agile advice we openly ignore: "daily standups must happen every day." We run two standups a week plus one backend-only meeting. From a pure development standpoint this is less than ideal — developers still need to communicate daily about where tasks stand. But from the stakeholder side it's the better trade: the PO doesn't really have time to attend a standup every day, and honestly only needs a couple of updates a week, not a daily one.

---

What's ideal is that developers just message each other more — has the PR been reviewed yet, is communication flowing freely between people who should be working closely together. In that sense the lack of a daily standup is more of a feature than a bug. A daily standup allows a failure mode: developers only communicate during that meeting, instead of raising blockers continually as they actually occur.

---

Agile is meant to be a starting point, not a finishing goal. You try things, see what works for your team in your context, and keep what works. Taking generic agile advice and assuming it applies in all situations is the real failure mode — it's the classic "silver bullet" problem: believing one thing will solve every problem everywhere. Agile is a set of principles and values that gives you a rough sense of good vs. bad, but it doesn't tell you exactly what to do in your situation. The consultant's "it depends" is closer to correct than we usually give it credit for. So the goal isn't to blindly follow the rules — it's to figure out how to break them.

---

One piece of generic advice I actually keep: retrospectives must happen every sprint, without fail. I believe in always having retros, regardless of whether things are going badly or not. A retro shouldn't only exist as a vent session, or a place to dump negativity. Having a retro when nothing much is going on, when everyone's in a good mood, helps spread that good feeling through the team and builds a more positive outlook in general.

---

The format of a "good mood" retro stays mostly the same — it's the tone that shifts to celebration. More thanks get shared, you talk about customers being happy with a recent feature you shipped, you say positive things about the company, about management, about the way you're working, or just look back on how far you've come.

---

None of these deviations were designed on purpose — they drift into place organically, through the retrospectives. One of the advantages of keeping the same team together for a while is that everyone discovers what ways of working people actually like and dislike. The retro becomes the mechanism for customizing agile to fit your team. It might be the very thing that keeps you from "doing stupid shit": if a practice doesn't make sense anymore, you raise it in the retro, and it may — or may not — get edited as a result.

---

People blindly following agile — "cargo culting" it — is a very real issue. The company feels stale, the features unimportant, and you fall into the trap of "crap on time." You story point, you do planning poker, you hand developers a variety of tasks, and come hell or high water they ship "on time." Except there's a ton of caveats hiding behind that word "shipped" — maybe it only shipped on their branch, not in production, or it shipped and no one ever tested it. Just checking boxes can quickly become a plague on a team that's hard to escape, and once communication breaks down, it's hard to get it back.

---

I would have said I "always worked in an agile environment" at previous jobs, but that was largely not true — not to the degree it is now. The "agile" I experienced before was really only embraced by me. Whenever the team as a whole interpreted something differently than I thought agile should work, the team won, at the end of the day. Which points at something true: you are the sum of your parts. If most of the team is jaded by agile, or just doesn't fundamentally understand the guiding principles, then you are not actually agile. You're the cargo cult.

---

What's different at my current company isn't that everyone suddenly bought into agile's principles — it's that leadership gave the team room to deviate. We're allowed to make reasonable edits to agile during the retrospective, provided they make sense and don't stray too far from "accepted" ways of working.

---

One of the more extreme asks I remember was a developer on another team wanting to change the kanban board. I didn't agree with it, and explained why the board was working as-is. That change would have disadvantaged a lot of people just to uphold the opinion of a single developer.

---

I've been that overly opinionated developer myself, many times. It takes a lot of humbling and learning to realize you're out of line, probably wrong, and need to just chill out on "improvements."

---

In school, a developer was talking about sockets and how a webpage works, in a way that was mostly not true. I jumped in and told him the browser mostly talks to the API over HTTP, not sockets. Someone who actually knew what they were talking about corrected me — sockets are just a layer underneath HTTP, and he was technically right. I didn't reply in the moment. I still didn't fully agree, but it was a public setting, and I didn't want an elongated, potentially heated debate where I end up on the wrong side of it. That's really what I'm talking about — being able to see that you could easily be wrong, and instead of digging your heels in, stepping back and trying to see the other person's point of view.

---

The kanban suggestion felt largely uninformed to me — I think he was mostly recoiling at being told what to do, which is a very human reaction. Though honestly, I can only see someone's outside behavior, not the reasoning behind it. His actual argument was that there was a mismatch between the activities the team was doing and what the board displayed, and that it could be redesigned to better match the work. The board had been designed in an earlier era of the team, but I still felt it was valid for the two things a board actually needs to show: where the work is, and what the blockers are.
